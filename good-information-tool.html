Good Information

A companion to I. J. Good's On the Principle of Total Evidence (1967)

What this tool is for

I. J. Good's 1967 paper On the Principle of Total Evidence is short, influential, and not especially user-friendly. Its central result is striking and important, but the presentation is compressed and the math can be off-putting. This tool is a companion to that paper: a way to see the result clearly, understand why it's true, and get a feel for what it's saying.

What Good proved

Given some background assumptions about rational choice, Good showed that getting extra information can never leave you worse off, and will often leave you better off. So, provided the information is free — no cost to acquire, no side effects — it's always rational to take it.

He also showed a stronger version: sometimes it's rational to pay for information. That part we'll set aside. The claim we're going to focus on is the one about free information: you should always accept it.

The crucial math

Here's the inequality at the heart of the result:

Σi P(ei) · maxa Σj P(sj | ei) · U(a, sj)  ≥  maxa Σj P(sj) · U(a, sj)

Notation note. U(a, sj) is Good's way of writing the payoff (utility) of taking action a when the world is in state sj. In the walkthroughs that follow, we'll write the same quantity as EV(a&sj) — the expected value of the action-and-state combination. Two notations, same thing. We come back to this at the end of "The math" tab, where Good's formula and the one we've built out of the walkthroughs sit side by side.

The left side is your expected utility if you get to hear the evidence before deciding. The right side is your expected utility if you have to decide without hearing it. Good's theorem says the left side is always at least as big as the right side.

Two things the result is assuming: (i) the information is free — there's no cost to acquiring it, and (ii) acquiring the information doesn't change the decision situation itself. The available actions, the payoffs, and the underlying probabilities are the same whether or not you got the evidence. Good's theorem is a claim about information that just slides in alongside the decision without disturbing it.

That's potentially intimidating and non-transparent, so we're going to unpack it — but not by staring at the symbols. We'll work through some concrete decisions first and build the intuition, then come back and see how the math encodes what we've already seen.

Before we start: the idea in plain English

The intuition the math is coding up is this: if you get some extra information, that'll put you in a position where you should expect to make better choices, with higher expected value. That's because the extra information can put you in a position to do a better job picking an action that's a good fit for the actual state of the world. So you should expect to do better in the decision situation where you have the information than in the situation where you don't.

More carefully: extra information will sometimes put you in a position where you should expect to make better choices, and will never put you in a position where you should expect to make worse ones.

That's the intuitive idea that the math is formalizing. Let's see how it works in a specific case.

Case 1: A Simple Betting Case

The Setup

Expected value of deciding without information:

You must pick one action to commit to. Its expected value depends on which state obtains — but you don't know which, so you weight by the probabilities.

That is:

max( EV(BetH), EV(BetT) )

(In this case, the max is trivial — both bets have the same EV.)

Unpacking each EV — weighting the payoff for each action by the probability of each state:

max( P(H)·EV(BetH&H) + P(T)·EV(BetH&T) , P(H)·EV(BetT&H) + P(T)·EV(BetT&T) )

= max( 0.5×$2 + 0.5×$0 , 0.5×$0 + 0.5×$2 )
= max( $1.00, $1.00 ) = $1.00

Expected value of deciding with information:

You get to pick a separate action for each possible state you might learn. Your overall expected value is the weighted average of these per-state best outcomes.

That is:

P(H)·max( EV(BetH&H), EV(BetT&H) ) + P(T)·max( EV(BetH&T), EV(BetT&T) )

No further unpacking needed here — when you know the state, EV(BetH&H) is simply the payoff for that action in that state.

= 0.5×max($2, $0) + 0.5×max($0, $2)
= 0.5×$2 + 0.5×$2 = $2.00

Notice the key structural difference: without information, you take the max across actions first, then nature resolves. With information, nature resolves first, then you pick the EV-maximizing action — separately for each state.

Expected value of deciding with partial information:

Now you learn which coin is going to be flipped before you act. You choose separately for each possible piece of evidence, and your overall EV is the weighted average of your per-evidence best outcomes.

That is:

P(coin1)·max( EV(BetH|coin1), EV(BetT|coin1) )
+ P(coin2)·max( EV(BetH|coin2), EV(BetT|coin2) )

Now we need to unpack each EV(BetH|coin1). Unlike Cases 1 and 2, knowing the evidence doesn't tell you the state — the coin still needs to be flipped. So each EV inside the max is itself a weighted sum:

P(coin1)·max(
  P(H|coin1)·EV(BetH&H) + P(T|coin1)·EV(BetH&T) ,
  P(H|coin1)·EV(BetT&H) + P(T|coin1)·EV(BetT&T) )
+ P(coin2)·max(
  P(H|coin2)·EV(BetH&H) + P(T|coin2)·EV(BetH&T) ,
  P(H|coin2)·EV(BetT&H) + P(T|coin2)·EV(BetT&T) )

Filling in the conditional probabilities — coin1 (heads-biased) gives P(H|coin1) = 0.75, P(T|coin1) = 0.25; coin2 (tails-biased) gives P(H|coin2) = 0.25, P(T|coin2) = 0.75:

0.5×max(
  0.75×$3 + 0.25×$0 ,
  0.75×$0 + 0.25×$2 )
+ 0.5×max(
  0.25×$3 + 0.75×$0 ,
  0.25×$0 + 0.75×$2 )

= 0.5×max( $2.25, $0.50 ) + 0.5×max( $0.75, $1.50 )
= 0.5×$2.25 + 0.5×$1.50 = $1.875

The structure hasn't changed: the with-information side still puts the max inside, choosing per-evidence-outcome. But something new has appeared — conditional probabilities. In Cases 1 and 2, evidence told you the state outright, so EV(BetH&H) collapsed to a payoff. Here, knowing the coin doesn't tell you the flip result. Each EV inside the max is itself a weighted sum, computed with updated probabilities.

What we've arrived at is a mathematical description of a general phenomenon: extra information lets you make better choices by reducing uncertainty about decision-relevant states. The limiting case is learning the state outright (Cases 1 and 2), but the same putting-you-in-a-better-position-to-tailor-your-actions-to-the-state thing happens even when the evidence doesn't take you all the way to certainty.

Generalizing

From this case to the general result:

Use ← → to step through.

The formula above describes a specific case: two possible actions (BetH and BetT), two possible states (Heads and Tails), and two possible pieces of evidence (coin1 and coin2). But the phenomenon is general — the same kind of thing is going to happen in more complicated cases, with more possible actions, more possible states, and more possible answers to the question you get the answer to before acting.

We need some new notation in order to write down the math that describes the general case.

To generalize, we need to use variables rather than names of specific actions, states, and pieces of evidence. And we need to allow for arbitrarily many of each. So we'll use:

a₁, a₂, …, aₙ for actions
s₁, s₂, …, sₘ for states
e₁, e₂, …, eₖ for evidence outcomes

Since we're allowing for cases with many actions, states, and evidence outcomes, we can't write the probability-weighted averages as lists the way we did before. In list form, the with-information side would look like:

P(e₁) · max over all actions a of [ P(s₁|e₁)·EV(a&s₁) + P(s₂|e₁)·EV(a&s₂) + ··· + P(sₘ|e₁)·EV(a&sₘ) ]
+ P(e₂) · max over all actions a of [ ··· ]
+ ···
+ P(eₖ) · max over all actions a of [ ··· ]

This is getting unwieldy. We need a more compact way to write "add up P(sⱼ|eᵢ)·EV(a&sⱼ) for every state sⱼ" and "add up across every evidence outcome eᵢ."

The standard way to write "add these up for each value of the variable" is sigma notation, using the Greek letter Σ (capital sigma). It works like this:

Σj P(sj|ei)·EV(a&sj)

This means: "for each state sj (s₁, s₂, …, sₘ), compute P(sj|ei)·EV(a&sj), then add them all up." The subscript j under the Σ tells you which variable is being ranged over.

Similarly, Σi means "add up for each evidence outcome ei," and maxa means "try every possible action a and take whichever gives the largest value."

Using sigma notation, the formula for the expected value of the decision without information looks like this:

maxa Σj P(sj) · EV(a&sj)

Reading from outside in: pick the action a that maximizes the sum, over all states sj, of the probability of that state times the payoff. This is exactly what we computed in every without-information walkthrough — choose one action, weight its payoffs by state probabilities, take the best.

The with-information side looks like this:

Σi P(ei) · maxa Σj P(sj|ei) · EV(a&sj)

Reading from outside in: for each possible evidence outcome ei, weight by its probability, then within that evidence outcome, pick the best action — where "best" now means maximizing a sum weighted by updated probabilities P(sj|ei).

The key structural difference is the same one we've seen throughout: the max is now inside the sum over evidence outcomes, not outside it. You get to choose per evidence outcome, not once for all of them.

Putting them together:

Σi P(ei) · maxa Σj P(sj|ei) · EV(a&sj)   ≥   maxa Σj P(sj) · EV(a&sj)

You can see that this is the generalization of the Case 3 inequality: replace the specific actions, states, and evidence outcomes with variables, and use sigma notation to sum over arbitrarily many of each. The structure is identical.

And the with-information EV must be greater than or equal to the without-information EV for the same reason it held in the three cases we worked through: the with-information side gets to choose a different action for each evidence outcome. The without-information side is forced to choose one action for all of them. The with-information side can always at least replicate the without-information strategy (by choosing the same action regardless of evidence), so it can never do worse — and it often does better.

Now compare what we just arrived at with the inequality from the first page — the one from Good's paper:

What we built:

Σi P(ei) · maxa Σj P(sj|ei) · EV(a&sj)  ≥  maxa Σj P(sj) · EV(a&sj)

Good's version:

Σi P(ei) · maxa Σj P(sj|ei) · U(a, sj)  ≥  maxa Σj P(sj) · U(a, sj)

These are the same inequality in slightly different notation. The only difference is that Good writes U(a, sj) where we write EV(a&sj) — both mean the value of taking action a when the world is in state sj. Good uses the traditional utility-function notation U(action, state); we've been writing the expected value of the action-and-state combination.

That's the result. The inequality that looked intimidating on the first page is exactly the general version of what you've been watching happen in the walkthroughs — written in compact notation.

1 / 7

Good's theorem says that inequality always holds. Getting information before deciding can never leave you worse off — and will usually leave you better off. It's not obvious! You might think extra information could make you second-guess yourself, or lead to worse decisions. But the math says no: if you use the information rationally, you can only benefit.

That conclusion rests on a specific assumption, though: that you care only about maximizing expected utility. If your preferences are more complex than that, the theorem might not apply. We'll come back to that.

It also rests on the setup assumption we flagged at the start: that acquiring the information doesn't change the decision situation. The actions, payoffs, and probabilities have to be the same whether or not you got the evidence. If the act of getting the information can itself shift any of these — e.g., by changing which actions are available, altering payoffs, or shifting the probabilities of the states — the inequality no longer has to hold.

And there's a related assumption baked into the whole setup: that your choice of action doesn't affect which state obtains. Betting on Heads doesn't make the coin more likely to come up Heads. The probabilities P(sⱼ) and P(sⱼ|eᵢ) are the same regardless of what you do. When that's not true — when what you do can shift the probabilities of the states themselves — the theorem as stated doesn't apply.

Putting a Number on It

Once you can compute the EV of a decision situation with and without information, you can put a precise number on how much the information is worth.

V(information) = EV(with info) − EV(without info)

In our coin-flip case: V(info) = $2.00 − $1.00 = $1.00. That's the most you should be willing to pay, in expectation, to learn the coin result before betting.

When Is Information Most Valuable?

Information is worthless when the same action is best regardless of the state. Example: choosing between mac and cheese vs. mud and broken glass. You always pick mac and cheese, so learning the "state" doesn't change your decision.

Information is most valuable when:

  1. Different states favor different actions, AND
  2. The states are roughly equally probable

What Good's Theorem Assumes

Good's Theorem proves a clean result: information never makes you worse at maximizing expected value. But this comes with crucial assumptions:

  1. Getting the information doesn't change the decision problem. The payoffs stay the same. Your options stay the same.
  2. You only care about maximizing expected utility. Beliefs matter only insofar as they guide behavior toward higher payoffs.

What if one of these breaks down? Then information might hurt you, even though Good's Theorem still holds.

Here's the key: Good is proving something narrowly true — information improves your ability to choose actions that maximize expected value. But there's much more to the value of information than that. Your beliefs matter for other reasons too: comfort, identity, relationships, aesthetic experience, autonomy. When we value information through that lens, the story changes.

Case 2: Identity Costs (The Shame Example)

A Variation on Wisconsin

Imagine you will feel terrible if you bet on Wisconsin with a credence lower than your stated belief. It feels dishonest, like you're gambling with money you don't actually believe in.

  • You state credence: 10% chance Wisconsin wins
  • You'd feel shame if you bet with credence < 10%
  • Shame cost: -$3 (adds to the decision calculation)

Without information: You believe Wisconsin 10%, so betting feels honest. No shame. You bet, EV = 0.1 × $7 + 0.9 × (-$1) + social = -$0.20 + social.

With information that Wisconsin loses (90% likely):

  • Your credence drops to 0% — you know they'll lose
  • If you bet anyway: -$1 (certain loss) + social - $3 (shame) = social - $4
  • If you don't bet: $0 (no social participation either)
  • Either way, you're worse off than if you'd never learned
With information (assume social value = $2): Learn W wins (10%): bet, get $7 + $2 = $9 Learn W loses (90%): best option is don't bet, get $0 EV = 0.1 × $9 + 0.9 × $0 = $0.90 Without information (social = $2): Bet at 10% credence (no shame): 0.1 × $7 + 0.9 × (-$1) + $2 = $1.80 Information COSTS you $0.90. You're better off not knowing!

The real issue: Once you learn Wisconsin will lose, your credence is forced to match reality. You can't maintain the comfortable 10% belief. Even if you choose not to bet, you've lost the innocence of your original belief.

Core insight: The shame/identity cost comes from the gap between your stated credence and the actual truth. Learning the truth forces you to update, and that update can be psychologically painful — separate from the betting decision.

Case 3: Spoiler Alerts (Pure Enjoyment vs. Accuracy)

The Movie Setup

  • You're about to watch a murder mystery movie.
  • A friend offers to tell you who the murderer is (free information).
  • Knowing the answer early lets you win bets with other friends about "whodunit": +$1 value
  • But knowing the answer drains the suspense and enjoyment from watching: -$3 cost

Don't Ask (Stay Ignorant)

Betting value from guessing wrong: $0 (You'll probably get it wrong or tie.) Enjoyment value: $3 Total: $3

Ask (Learn the Spoiler)

Betting value from knowing: $1 (You win the whodunit bets.) Enjoyment value: $0 (No suspense, no surprise.) Total: $1

The information is worth -$2 to you. Good's Theorem says knowing the answer improves your betting accuracy. It does! You'd bet better with the information. But you care about more than betting accuracy. You care about enjoying a movie.

The Lesson: Good's Theorem is technically correct — the information improves your ability to choose winning bets. But you don't want to optimize for winning bets; you want to maximize enjoyment. These are in conflict. Learning the answer is bad for you, even though it's good for your betting.

When Is Information Genuinely Bad?

Let's separate out the sources of value:

Total value of information = Behavior-guidance value + Other values

Behavior-guidance value is what Good's Theorem covers. It's always ≥ 0. Information always helps you choose actions that maximize expected value.

Other values can be negative. Examples:

  • Comfort: Ignorance can be bliss. Learning uncomfortable truths costs you peace of mind.
  • Identity: Learning can force you to update a cherished self-image.
  • Relationships: Knowing something might obligate you to act in ways that damage friendships (like having to tell someone bad news).
  • Enjoyment: Spoilers. Mysteries. Surprises. Learning ruins them.
  • Autonomy: Some people care about the freedom to believe what they want, separate from accuracy.

When the "other values" cost exceeds the behavior-guidance value, learning the truth makes you worse off. Good is right. Your betting improves. But you care about things beyond betting.

The Big Picture

Good's Theorem: Free information always improves your ability to choose actions that maximize expected utility. This is mathematically proven and deeply important.

But here's what often gets lost: guiding behavior is not the only thing beliefs are for.

Our beliefs matter because they:

  • Guide our behavior ← Good's Theorem applies here
  • Make us feel comfortable
  • Constitute our identity
  • Connect us to communities
  • Shape our aesthetic experiences
  • Give us a sense of autonomy and control

When information threatens one of these non-behavioral values, learning the truth might make you worse off — even though Good is right that your decision-making improves.

This doesn't mean Good's Theorem is wrong. It just means that the theorem answers a narrower question than we often need. It says, "How much will this information help you choose the best action?" But sometimes we should be asking, "Will knowing this make my life better?" Those aren't always the same thing.

For Further Thought: Can you think of examples from your own life where you've avoided information because learning it would make you unhappy, even though it would improve your decision-making? What does that tell you about what you really value?
