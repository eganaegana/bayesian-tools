Problem-Solving Methods vs. Theory Choice

The Methods Framework

A scientific community faces a problem that might be solvable by either of two methods. Each method has an intrinsic prospect ρ — the probability it can deliver the answer, even with unlimited scientists. The community's probability of success is:

P(success) = ρA · f(nA) + ρB · f(nB)

Two factors per term: how promising is the method (ρ), and what fraction of that promise is realized by current effort (f(n)).

The Theory-Choice Framework

Now the community faces two competing theories, exactly one of which is true. This introduces an extra layer of uncertainty: even if a theory is true, its remaining anomalies might resist resolution. Each theory has a probability q of being the true theory (qA + qB = 1), and both theories share a tractability parameter p — the probability that the true theory's problems can be overcome with enough effort. Expected community success becomes:

E[success] = qA · p · f(nA) + qB · p · f(nB)

Three factors per term: is the theory true (q), are its problems solvable (p), and have the scientists solved them (f(n))?

What Changes?

The core tension is the same: individually rational scientists all pile onto the more promising option (higher ρ in methods, higher q in theory choice), but the community does better by maintaining diversity. But the theory-choice framing sharpens the bite. A scientist who works on the less probable theory isn't just choosing a less promising method — they're knowingly betting against the evidence about which theory is true. That's a harder bullet to bite individually, even though the community benefits from their doing so.

The tractability parameter p adds a uniform ceiling: even with infinite scientists, each theory's problems can only be overcome with probability p. When p is low, the absolute stakes are lower (the community is less likely to succeed regardless), but the relative advantage of diversification stays the same. Because p is shared between theories, it doesn't affect where the optimal distribution falls — only how much total probability is at stake.

In the probability space diagram above, theory-choice mode shows this as a tractability ceiling: a horizontal line at height p, above which lies the "intractable" zone that no amount of effort can reach. The filled region represents the probability the community has actually captured, and the gap between the filled region and the ceiling represents unrealized potential that more scientists could capture.
