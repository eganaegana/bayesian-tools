About This Model ▼

This simulation demonstrates Schelling's segregation model from his 1978 book Micromotives and Macrobehavior.

The mechanism: Each person has a mild preference — they'd rather not be in a dining hall where their gender comprises less than their personal comfort threshold (averaging 33%, but varying from person to person). This is a very modest preference; nobody wants to force others out or explicitly segregate.

What happens: Starting from a slightly uneven distribution, a cascade emerges. The most sensitive people leave first because they're below their threshold. This shifts the balance, making the next most sensitive people uncomfortable. They leave too. The cascade continues, and the halls separate almost completely — even though nobody wanted segregation.

The insight: Individual rationality and mild preferences can produce outcomes nobody intended. Macrobehavior emerges from micromotives.

Try it: Leave the defaults (33% average threshold, 30% men in Hall A), press Setup, then Run. Watch the cascade unfold one person at a time. Then try lowering the threshold or the spread to see how the outcome changes.
